What Am I Looking At?

The screen shows a Tonnetz (German for "tone network") — a grid of dots where each dot is a musical note (C, D, E, etc.). The dots are arranged so that notes which sound good together are always right next to each other.

The key insight: every chord is a triangle. Three notes that form a chord will always be three dots that make a triangle on the grid. This means you can literally see the harmony of what you're singing.

The grid repeats in every direction (like wallpaper), so the same note appears in multiple places. This is normal — it's how the geometry works.

What Happens When I Sing

  1. You sing a note — the microphone detects the pitch
  2. A dot lights up blue on the grid — that's your note
  3. When you move to a new note, the old dot fades to grey briefly (this is the "ghost" — it shows where you just were)
  4. If 3 notes form a chord, a colored triangle fills in between them
  5. The key display at the top analyzes your singing over time and tells you what key you're in
Tip: Sing slowly and clearly at first. The microphone needs a steady tone to detect your pitch accurately. You'll see the status bar show the detected note name, frequency, and a "clarity" number — higher clarity means a cleaner detection.

Color Guide — What Every Color Means

Here's a complete reference for everything you'll see on screen:

The Dots (Notes)

ColorWhat It Means
Dark blue Active note — this is the note you're singing right now. The dot fills dark blue and its label turns white.
Grey Ghost note — you just stopped singing this note. It fades away after about half a second. Helps you see where you just were.
Blue ring Diatonic scale note — once the system detects your key, all 7 notes of that scale get a subtle blue ring. These are the "home" notes of your key — notes that will generally sound consonant with your melody.
Green ring Suggested next note — when "Suggest" is enabled, notes that would complete a chord with what you're currently singing get a green ring. These are harmonically strong choices for where to go next.

The Triangles (Chords)

ColorWhat It Means
Light yellow Major chord — three notes you're singing (or that are ringing) form a major triad. Major chords sound bright and happy. The label on the triangle shows the chord name in UPPERCASE (e.g., C, G, F).
Cream Minor chord — three notes form a minor triad. Minor chords sound darker or more emotional. The label shows the chord name in lowercase (e.g., c, a, e).
Light blue wash Nearby chords (Regions feature) — when enabled, triangles near an active chord get a faint blue wash. Brighter blue = one step away from a chord you're playing. Fainter blue = two steps away. These are chords that share notes with what you're singing, making them natural choices for accompaniment.

The Trace (Chord History)

ColorWhat It Means
Orange triangle Detected chord — when you sing 3 notes that form a chord, the trace draws a filled orange triangle with the chord name (e.g., "C" or "Am") labeled inside.
Thin orange line Chord progression — a faint line connects the centers of your detected chords in order, showing the harmonic path of your song.
Red dot Latest note — the most recent note or chord in your trace is highlighted bright red so you can see where you are.
Small dim dot Passing tone — a note that didn't form part of a chord. These are normal — melodies have passing tones, neighbor tones, and other non-chord notes between the structural harmony.

The Triangles: Chords Made Visible

This is the heart of what makes the Tonnetz useful for a singer. On the standard grid:

  • Every set of 3 adjacent dots forms a triangle
  • Every triangle is a chord (technically a "triad" — a 3-note chord)
  • Triangles pointing one way are major chords yellow
  • Triangles pointing the other way are minor chords cream

When do they light up?

A triangle fills with color when all 3 of its notes are active at the same time. For a singer, this usually means:

  • You're singing one note, and 2 others are still "ghosting" (fading) — all 3 briefly overlap
  • Or you're singing while an instrument plays the other notes
  • Or the sustain pedal is holding previous notes

Why do the triangles matter?

They tell an accompanist exactly what chord fits your melody right now. If you see a yellow triangle light up with "G" in it, that means your recent notes outline a G major chord — so the band or pianist can play G major and it will sound right.

What about different trichords?

The grid can be reconfigured to use different interval structures (called "trichords"). The standard [3,4,5] grid uses minor thirds and major thirds — the intervals that build traditional Western chords. But TonnetzViz can also analyze your singing and automatically switch to whichever trichord best fits the intervals in your melody. When the grid changes, the triangles still work the same way — they just represent different types of 3-note structures.

Key Detection

After you've sung about 20 notes, the system starts displaying the detected key at the top of the screen (e.g., "Probably G major" or "Probably E minor").

How it works

  1. It keeps a rolling window (about 15 seconds) of every note you sing
  2. It builds a histogram — which notes appear most, and for how long
  3. It compares this against known key profiles (what notes are typical for each key)
  4. The best match is displayed, but only after it's been stable for several analysis cycles (to avoid flickering between keys)

What the key tells you

  • For the accompanist: the key tells you what chords to draw from. In G major, the natural chords are G, Am, Bm, C, D, Em, and F#dim.
  • For the singer: the blue-ringed notes on the grid show you the 7 notes "in your key." If you tend to sing those notes, you're staying in key. Notes without the ring are chromatic (outside the key) — not wrong, just more colorful.
Tip: If you're singing a song that changes key (a modulation), the display will eventually catch up. It takes a few seconds to stabilize on the new key, which is intentional — it avoids false alarms from passing notes.

The Trace: Your Song's Chord Map

When the Trace feature is turned on, TonnetzViz records your melody and builds a visual map of the chords your singing outlines over time.

What you'll see

  • Orange triangles with chord names — every time your last 3 distinct notes form a chord on the current grid, they collapse into a labeled triangle (e.g., "C", "Am", "G")
  • A thin connecting line between the triangles — this shows the order of your chord progression
  • Small dim dots for notes that didn't form a chord — these are passing tones in your melody
  • A bright red marker on the most recent entry

How chord detection works for singers

Unlike a piano where you can press 3 keys at once, a singer produces notes one at a time. The trace watches your notes in sequence:

  1. You sing a C — a dot appears
  2. You sing an E — another dot appears
  3. You sing a G — the system recognizes that C, E, G form a C major chord. The 3 dots collapse into a single orange "C" triangle

This happens automatically. The detection only looks at consecutive notes (it won't skip over a triad boundary to combine distant notes), so the results reflect the actual flow of your melody.

Replay

After singing, you can press the Replay button to watch a playhead animate through your chord progression. This is useful for reviewing what you sang and seeing the harmonic motion on the grid.

Using This for Accompaniment

This is the core use case: a singer performs, and a musician watches TonnetzViz to build accompaniment in real time. Here's what to watch:

1. Wait for the key

Give the singer 10–15 seconds. The key display will stabilize to something like "Probably D major." This is your tonal center — the home chord and scale for the song.

2. Watch the triangles

As the singer continues, watch which triangles light up on the grid:

  • Yellow triangles = major chords (brighter, happier)
  • Cream triangles = minor chords (darker, more emotional)
  • The chord labels on each triangle tell you the name

3. Use the regions

Turn on Regions to see blue-washed triangles near the active chord. These are neighboring chords that share notes — they'll sound smooth if you move to them. Great for adding passing chords or fills.

4. Read the trace

If Trace is on, the orange triangles and connecting line show the singer's chord progression over time. This helps you spot patterns — if the singer keeps going C → Am → F → G, you know the progression and can anticipate the next chord.

5. Follow the suggestions

Turn on Suggest to see green rings around notes that would complete a chord with what's currently sounding. If you're playing an instrument alongside, these tell you what note to add to create harmony.

Example Scenarios

Singer performs "Let It Be"

The singer starts. After a few phrases, the key display shows "Probably C major." The diatonic notes (C, D, E, F, G, A, B) get blue rings on the grid.

As the melody moves through C–E–G, a yellow "C" triangle lights up. Then the melody moves to A–C–E, and a cream "a" triangle appears. The trace shows: C → Am → F → G — the classic progression, visible as a chain of labeled orange triangles.

The accompanist sees this pattern and plays along confidently.

Singer improvises in an unfamiliar mode

The singer uses notes from the Dorian mode. At first, the key display might flicker between minor and major. After several phrases it settles on "Probably D minor" (Dorian is close to natural minor).

The accompanist watches the triangles: mostly minor triads lighting up, with occasional major ones. The regions feature shows which nearby chords share notes with whatever is active, helping the accompanist pick chords that fit the modal sound.

Auto-trichord adapts to the music

The singer's melody uses intervals that don't fit the standard [3,4] grid well (perhaps lots of 4ths and 2nds, like a quartal/sus2 sound). With Auto-trichord enabled, the system detects this and switches the grid to [2,5] (whole tone + perfect 4th). Now the triangles represent sus2/quartal structures instead of traditional triads, and the chord labels reflect the actual harmony of the singing.

Controls Quick Reference

ControlWhat It Does
Start/Stop Microphone Begin or end listening to your voice. Select your mic from the dropdown first.
Trace Toggle the chord history trail. When on, it records and displays the chords your melody outlines.
Clear Trajectory Erase the current trace and start fresh.
Replay Animate a playhead through the recorded trace. Speed can be adjusted (Slow / Normal / Fast).
Regions Show nearby chords with a blue wash. Great for finding related chords.
Suggest Show green rings on notes that would complete a chord. Helps find harmonically strong notes.
Key Analysis Enabled automatically with the microphone. Shows the detected key at the top of the screen.
Auto-trichord Let the system choose the best grid intervals to match your singing.
Ghost duration How long released notes stay visible (default 500ms). Increase this to see more overlap between consecutive notes, which helps more triangles light up when singing one note at a time.
Zoom (+/−) Mouse wheel or +/− keys. Zoom in to see fewer, larger triangles, or out to see more of the grid.
Panic Turn off all notes immediately. Useful if something gets stuck.
Tip for singers: Increase the ghost duration to 1000ms or more. Since you sing one note at a time, a longer ghost means your previous notes stay visible longer, giving the system more chance to show complete chord triangles. This is the single most useful setting for vocal use.
